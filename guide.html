YOUR GUIDE TO DOWNHILL MTB

Everything you need to know before dropping in

What is Downhill MTB? Essential Equipment Safety First Choosing Your First Destination What to Expect Fitness & Preparation

Downhill mountain biking (DH) is gravity-fed riding at its most intense. Riders are transported to the top of a mountain by lift, gondola, or vehicle, then descend purpose-built trails featuring jumps, drops, rock gardens, and bermed corners. It's the Formula 1 of mountain biking -- specialized bikes, full protective gear, and tracks designed to push the limits of what's rideable.

The sport emerged in the 1970s in California but has found its spiritual home in the mountains of Europe, where alpine infrastructure (ski lifts repurposed for summer) created the perfect playground for gravity riders.

The right equipment is the difference between a great day and a hospital visit. Here's what you need, ranked by importance.

Full-Face Helmet

Non-negotiable. Must be certified (CPSC/EN 1078 minimum, ASTM DH preferred). Replace after any impact. A good helmet is your single most important investment.

Body Armor

Back protector, chest, and elbow/forearm protection. Look for CE Level 2 rated armor. D3O and similar impact materials offer great protection-to-weight ratio.

Gloves

Full-finger gloves with palm padding. Essential for grip, protection against branches, and crash slides. Replace when they lose grip texture.

Goggles

Protect eyes from mud, dust, rocks, and branches. Wide field of vision is crucial. Carry a clear lens for low-light conditions and a tinted lens for sunshine.

DH Bike

200mm+ front and rear suspension, slack geometry, 27.5" or 29" wheels. Most bike parks offer high-quality rental bikes if you don't own one -- this is a great option for beginners.

Flat Pedal Shoes

Stiff, flat-soled shoes with sticky rubber compounds (like Five Ten Stealth). The grip on flat pedals is essential for confidence and quick foot-off recovery.

Knee Pads

Your knees are exposed and vulnerable. Hard-shell knee pads with D3O or similar impact foam. Must stay in place during a crash -- test the fit before riding.

Riding Clothing

Durable, flexible shorts or pants and a jersey. DH-specific clothing is designed to fit over armor. Avoid anything loose that could snag on the bike or branches.

Golden Rule

Ride within your ability. The mountain will always be there tomorrow. Progress gradually and never let peer pressure push you beyond what feels safe.

Trail Etiquette

  1. Downhill riders have priority -- uphill riders should yield on DH-designated trails
  2. Don't stop on blind corners -- if you need to stop, move completely off the trail
  3. Call out when overtaking -- a clear "rider behind" or "on your left" prevents surprises
  4. Don't cut trails or ride off-piste -- it damages the environment and creates hazards
  5. Report trail damage -- fallen trees, washed-out sections, or dangerous conditions

If You Crash

  1. Stay still and assess -- don't jump up immediately
  2. Check for serious injury: can you move all limbs? Any numbness or severe pain?
  3. If injured, stay put and signal for help. Most bike parks have trail patrol
  4. If okay, move yourself and your bike off the trail before other riders arrive
  5. Inspect your helmet -- any impact means it must be replaced

Weather Awareness

Mountain weather changes fast. Wet roots and rocks become treacherous. Lightning at altitude is genuinely dangerous. Check forecasts before riding, carry a lightweight rain jacket, and don't hesitate to call it early if conditions deteriorate. A fun day cut short beats a dangerous day pushed too far.

A typical day at a bike park follows a rhythm. Here's what to expect on your first visit.

08:00

Arrive & Gear Up

Arrive early for parking and gear setup. Check bike, air up tires, adjust suspension settings. Apply sunscreen, fill water bottles.

09:00

First Lifts

Most lifts open at 9am. Queue early for the first gondola. Start with a warm-up run on an easy trail to get your flow.

10:00

Morning Session

3-4 runs on your chosen trails. Focus on progression -- each run, push a little further. Take photos. Drink water between runs.

12:30

Lunch Break

Mountain restaurants or packed lunch. Rest your arms. Refuel properly -- you're burning more energy than you think. Share stories with other riders.

14:00

Afternoon Session

2-3 more runs. Fatigue sets in -- this is when crashes happen. If your concentration dips, ride easier trails or call it a day. Quality over quantity.

16:30

Wind Down

Last lift usually around 16:30-17:00. Clean and inspect your bike. Check for any mechanical issues. Stretch while your muscles are warm.

18:00

Apres-Ride

Find a local bar or restaurant. Compare bruises, plan tomorrow's routes, and soak in the post-ride buzz. You've earned it.

Don't underestimate the physical demands of downhill. You may not be pedaling uphill, but your body is working hard to absorb impacts, control the bike, and maintain position. A day of DH riding is genuinely exhausting.

Key Muscle Groups

Forearms & Grip

The number one limiter for new riders. Gripping the bars and pulling the brake levers for extended descents causes intense forearm fatigue ("arm pump"). Train with grip strengtheners and dead hangs.

Core

Your core stabilizes everything. It transfers force between upper and lower body, helps absorb impacts, and keeps you balanced on the bike. Planks, dead bugs, and rotational exercises are gold.

Legs & Glutes

Standing on the pedals for minutes at a time in the "attack position" demands leg endurance. Squats, lunges, and wall sits build the specific strength you need.

Shoulders & Back

Absorbing impacts and controlling the front end loads your upper body. Push-ups, rows, and shoulder stability exercises prepare you for a full day of descending.

Training Tips

  • Start training 6-8 weeks before your trip for meaningful adaptation
  • Focus on grip endurance: farmer's carries, dead hangs, wrist curls
  • Ride your regular bike as much as possible -- time on any bike helps
  • Yoga or stretching improves the flexibility you need for the attack position
  • Cardio matters even though you're going downhill -- interval training is most relevant

Recovery

Multi-day riding trips demand good recovery practices. Stretch after each session, stay hydrated (altitude increases fluid loss), eat protein-rich meals, and sleep well. Consider a rest day mid-trip to explore the area on foot -- your body and your riding will thank you.
